I received my Ph.D. degree at the University of Central Florida working at the Wearable Engineering and Assistive Robotics (WEAR) Laboratory under Dr. Joon-Hyuk Park. My research focuses on **developing wearable robots to assist and enhance human physical performance**. Recent advancements in sensors, electronics, materials, and power sources have paved the way for innovative wearable robots designed to aid, enable, or augment human activities. Despite these advancements, several challenges still hinder the effectiveness and practicality of current wearable robots. Fundamental questions remain unanswered: How can we create wearable robots that are both effective and user-friendly? How can we develop human-centric wearable robots that incorporate the human element into their control systems? How can we ensure the safety of subjects while developing novel wearable robots? During my Ph.D. training, my major research projects focused on developing a myoelectric bilateral cable-driven exosuit for bilateral upper body augmentation and investigating the implementation of deep reinforcement learning in a predictive neuromusculoskeletal simulator.

Design and Validation of an Innovative Cable-Driven Upper Body Exosuit (CUBE)
======
My research on designing and validating an exosuit has focused on bilateral upper body augmentation and rehabilitation, incorporating a cable-driven system and a myoelectric control system. 

Upper limb exoskeletons are widely used to prevent work-related musculoskeletal disorders (WMSDs) in daily and industrial activities. However, they are often bulky, require precise alignment with biological joints, and suffer from high inertia due to rigid linkages, limiting their adoption. To address these issues, upper limb exosuits have been developed, but most current models provide only single-degree-of-freedom and unilateral assistance or rehabilitation. Their control schemes, typically compensates the interactive force measured by force sensor, fail to integrate seamlessly with the user’s movements. During my doctoral training, I focused on designing and validating a more versatile upper limb Exosuit controlled by the user's movement intentions.

![CUBE Exosuit](/images/CUBE.png)

In this project, my research sought to answer these fundamental questions: (i) How can we facilitate efficient human-machine interactions of wearable robots to maximize the benefits? (ii) What needs to be implemented to design and control a human-centric wearable robot in order to make it more adaptive and intelligent in response to changes in the user-, task- and environment-specific parameters? To address these questions, I developed the mechanical and electrical system of a modular four-degrees-of-freedom bilateral cable-driven upper body exosuit (CUBE) with minimally rigid architecture to develop. I also developed the software system of the CUBE exosuit on the LabVIEW. Besides, the CUBE incorporated on-board surface electromyography (sEMG) sensors to detect the human’s motion intention and implements proportional myoelectric control system to ensure real-time control through human’s motion intention with a limited computational cost. Furthermore, I designed the experimental protocol to validate the CUBE exosuit under the pick-and-place task with forearm and eye-level screwing task, the human-subject research included 30 subject which is still ongoing.

Deep Reinforcement Learning-based Auto-tuner for Internal Model of Human’s Central Neuron System
======
Another of my major research projects involves using deep reinforcement learning to auto-tune the internal model of the human central nervous system, simulating muscle activation.

Myoelectric control systems, which detect human motion intentions, have emerged as promising methods for controlling wearable robots like exoskeletons and prosthetics, enabling efficient and intelligent human-robot interactions. However, the inherent uncertainties in the human neuromusculoskeletal system challenge the robustness and safety of these control systems. Neuromusculoskeletal simulation has become an effective way to validate myoelectric control systems, minimizing potential risks to users. Currently, most simulations for this purpose use an internal model tuned by experimental data and prior knowledge. 

![DRL](/images/DRL.png)

In this work, I designed a novel deep reinforcement learning-based auto-tuner for the internal model of human’s central neuron system. Firstly, I built an upper limb neuromusculoskeletal simulation which utilizes the internal model in the Mujoco physics engine by using the MyoSuite model. The neuromusculoskeletal simulation can simulate the flexion and extension motion of the elbow joint and assistance of an exoskeleton. Then, I implemented the deep deterministic policy gradient algorithm to tune the internal model used to generate muscle activations. Compares the conventional methods used to tune the internal model, the deep reinforcement learning-based auto-tuner did not require any experimental data and prior knowledge of tuning procedure. Furthermore, this work also demonstrated the deep reinforcement learning-based auto-tuner of internal model as an efficient approach to tune the internal model of central neuron system. Additionally, I also benchmarked the internal model tuned by deep reinforcement learning algorithm with the deep reinforcement learning agent which is trained to output muscle activation. The results showed that the neuromusculoskeletal simulation controlled by the muscle activation generated from internal model tuned by deep reinforcement algorithm has better accuracy and less responding time.

The ongoing work of this project is to implement a more data efficient deep reinforcement algorithm such as proximal policy gradient algorithm to tune the internal model for multiple degree-of-freedom motion of upper extremities, and implement transfer reinforcement learning algorithm to extend the auto-tuned internal model from only support simple motion such as elbow flexion/extension to motions in the activities of daily life such as reaching a specific target.

A Cable-driven Ankle Perturbation System to Study Fall Mechanics from Slips and Trips
======
This work is a collaborative project to design and build a cable-driven ankle perturbation system and implement it to collect data on falls caused by slips or trips.

Nowadays, falls pose a significant threat to the health and safety of older adults globally. To mitigate this risk, it is essential to comprehend the biomechanics of non-steady state locomotion. By replicating real-world fall conditions in a controlled laboratory setting, we can safely and systematically investigate the balance control mechanisms in both healthy and fall-prone populations. This approach underpins the development of a cable-driven ankle perturbation system, designed to reliably induce random, unanticipated gait perturbations in a consistent and repeatable manner.

![DRL](/images/FallFES.png)

In this study, we designed a protective frame for human subjects. To ensure the structural integrity and optimize the design of the frame, we performed finite element analysis. The ankle perturbation is triggered by a cable-driven mechatronic system that can induce mechanical perturbations during walking. The system is designed to apply a pulling force to the stance leg using a brushless DC motor and a braking force to the swing leg using an electromagnetic brake to create slip and trip perturbations, respectively. The slip perturbation intensity is controlled by adjusting the pulling force magnitude, while the trip perturbation intensity is modulated by varying the brake activation duration. To control the timing of the perturbation onset, a gait segmentation method based on real-time plantar pressure measurement is used. The system is configured with a treadmill and an inertially fixed rigid frame, made from modular aluminum T-framing. Additionally, a full-body harness is attached to the frame's overhead beam to support the subject's body weight in the event of a fall to prevent any ground impact.

Ongoing Research and Future Directions
======
The two major research projects during my Ph.D. training represent significant steps toward developing a human-centric wearable robot and its control system. First, the development of the CUBE exosuit demonstrated that a portable upper limb exosuit could be versatile by incorporating assistance for different types of motion. Second, the modular design of the CUBE exosuit provided a platform for further research on optimizing the design and control system of an upper body exosuit, maximizing human benefits, and enhancing adaptability and intelligence in response to user-, task-, and environment-specific parameters. Furthermore, the successful implementation of deep reinforcement learning for tuning the internal model of the central nervous system in motor control within a neuromusculoskeletal simulation offers a novel approach that reduces the reliance on empirical data and prior tuning knowledge. This advancement simplifies the development of neuromusculoskeletal simulations for validating novel control systems of wearable robots, allowing researchers to focus on design and validation rather than simulation tuning.

However, my Ph.D. research projects still have limitations. The current version of the CUBE exosuit only supports shoulder and elbow flexion, with actuator output directly transmitted to the end-effector through artificial tendons. We are developing the next generation of the CUBE, which will include additional artificial tendons to assist extension motions and a parallel elastic actuation system to improve energy efficiency and safety.

Additionally, the deep reinforcement learning-based auto-tuner for the internal model of the human central nervous system has only been validated on a neuromusculoskeletal simulator supporting elbow flexion and extension. One of the key benefits of deep reinforcement learning is its capability to handle complex and high-dimensional state and action spaces. Therefore, a future extension involves applying the deep reinforcement learning-based auto-tuner to a full-functional, multiple degree-of-freedom upper extremities neuromusculoskeletal simulation. This auto-tuner should not only be validated on basic joint motions but also on various activities of daily living, such as reaching for an object by hand.
